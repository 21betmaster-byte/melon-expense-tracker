Managing money as a couple is fundamentally different from managing it alone. You need to track who paid for what, figure out fair splits, settle up without friction, and ideally do all of this without turning your relationship into an accounting exercise.

There are dozens of finance apps out there, but most are built for individuals or friend groups. Couples have unique needs: shared budgets, income-based splitting, recurring expense tracking, and the ability to see your financial picture together without merging bank accounts.

We tested seven popular apps that couples commonly use for expense splitting and financial management. Here is an honest breakdown of each one, including where they shine and where they fall short.

## What Couples Actually Need in an Expense App

Before diving into the comparison, here is what matters most for couples:

- **Easy expense logging** — If it takes more than 10 seconds to log a purchase, you will stop doing it.
- **Flexible splitting options** — 50/50, income-based proportional splits, or custom percentages.
- **Running balance** — A clear view of who owes whom at any point.
- **Shared categories and budgets** — Joint visibility into where money goes.
- **Settlement tracking** — A way to mark debts as settled without confusion.
- **Privacy balance** — Shared visibility on joint expenses while maintaining autonomy over personal spending.

With those criteria in mind, let us look at each app.

## 1. Melon

**Best for:** Couples who want a purpose-built expense splitting and tracking tool

Melon was designed from day one for couples. It is not a group expense splitter repurposed for two people, and it is not a full banking platform. It sits in the sweet spot: simple enough to use daily, powerful enough to handle the real-world complexity of shared finances.

**Key features:**
- One-tap expense logging with automatic partner notification
- Income-based proportional splitting calculated automatically
- Running balance that shows who owes whom in real time
- Shared expense categories with monthly summaries
- Settlement tracking with history
- Works as a progressive web app — no app store download needed

**Pricing:** Free to get started

**Strengths:** Melon is laser-focused on the couples use case. The proportional splitting feature is particularly useful for partners with different incomes, since it calculates fair shares based on your [income ratio](/blog/fair-expense-splitting-income-gap) without requiring manual math. The interface is clean and fast, which matters when you are logging expenses multiple times a day.

**Limitations:** Melon does not connect directly to bank accounts for automatic transaction import. You log expenses manually, which some couples actually prefer for awareness and control, but others may miss the automation.

## 2. Splitwise

**Best for:** Couples who also frequently split costs with friends and roommates

Splitwise is the most well-known expense splitting app, and for good reason. It handles group expenses exceptionally well, making it a natural choice if you split costs with roommates, travel companions, or friend groups in addition to your partner.

**Key features:**
- Group and pairwise expense tracking
- Simplify debts algorithm that minimizes the number of payments needed
- Receipt scanning
- Currency conversion for travel
- Integration with Venmo and PayPal for settlement

**Pricing:** Free with ads; Splitwise Pro at $4.99/month removes ads and adds receipt scanning, charts, and currency conversion.

**Strengths:** Splitwise is mature and reliable. If you travel frequently or have an active social life involving shared costs, it handles multi-person splitting better than anyone.

**Limitations:** Splitwise was built for friend groups, not couples. It lacks income-based proportional splitting, shared budgets, and the kind of running-balance clarity that couples need. The interface can feel cluttered when you only care about two-person splits. It also does not offer joint financial planning tools.

## 3. Honeydue

**Best for:** Couples who want to link bank accounts and see all transactions together

Honeydue markets itself as the finance app for couples. It connects to both partners' bank accounts and aggregates transactions in one view. You can comment on each other's transactions, set budgets, and chat within the app.

**Key features:**
- Bank account linking with automatic transaction import
- Joint and individual account visibility
- Bill reminders and due date tracking
- In-app chat
- Monthly budget setting by category

**Pricing:** Free

**Strengths:** If you want a birds-eye view of all household finances in one place, Honeydue delivers. The transaction commenting feature is genuinely useful — a quick note on a charge is much better than a "what was that $47 at Target?" text.

**Limitations:** The expense splitting functionality is basic. Honeydue is more of a financial visibility tool than a splitting tool. It shows you what is happening but does not actively help you calculate who owes what based on income differences. Some users have also reported reliability issues with bank syncing.

## 4. Zeta

**Best for:** Couples who want a joint bank account with built-in budgeting

Zeta goes beyond tracking — it offers an actual joint bank account (through a banking partner) alongside budgeting and expense management tools. It is designed for couples who want to partially or fully merge their finances.

**Key features:**
- Joint bank account with no fees
- Individual and shared account views
- Automated savings goals
- Bill splitting and tracking
- Financial planning tools

**Pricing:** Free for the basic account

**Strengths:** If you are ready to open a joint account and want budgeting tools built right in, Zeta is a compelling option. The ability to have both joint and individual views is well-executed.

**Limitations:** You need to open a Zeta bank account to access the full feature set, which is a bigger commitment than just downloading an app. Couples who prefer to keep separate banks but still want to split expenses fairly may find Zeta too heavy-handed. The splitting features are also less granular than dedicated splitting tools.

## 5. Goodbudget

**Best for:** Couples who love the envelope budgeting method

Goodbudget is a digital version of the classic envelope system, where you allocate money into category "envelopes" and spend from them until they are empty. It supports shared households with synced envelopes across devices.

**Key features:**
- Digital envelope budgeting
- Shared envelopes that sync between partners
- Debt tracking and payoff planning
- Reports and spending analysis
- Web and mobile access

**Pricing:** Free for up to 10 envelopes; Plus plan at $10/month for unlimited envelopes, multiple devices, and more.

**Strengths:** If the envelope method clicks for you, Goodbudget is the best digital implementation. The shared envelopes make it easy for couples to budget together in real time.

**Limitations:** Goodbudget is a budgeting tool, not an expense splitting tool. It does not calculate who owes whom or support proportional splitting. If your main need is figuring out fair expense splits rather than envelope-based budgeting, this is not the right fit. The free tier is also quite limited.

## 6. YNAB (You Need A Budget)

**Best for:** Couples who want a comprehensive, proactive budgeting system

YNAB is widely considered the gold standard of personal budgeting apps. Its "give every dollar a job" philosophy helps couples allocate income intentionally. It supports shared budgets and can connect to bank accounts for transaction import.

**Key features:**
- Zero-based budgeting methodology
- Bank account syncing
- Goal tracking with target dates
- Age of money metric
- Extensive educational resources and workshops

**Pricing:** $14.99/month or $109/year (34-day free trial)

**Strengths:** YNAB changes how you think about money. The methodology is powerful, and couples who commit to it often see dramatic improvements in their financial health. The shared budget means both partners work from the same plan.

**Limitations:** YNAB has a steep learning curve. It requires significant time investment to set up and maintain. It is also not designed for expense splitting at all — there is no concept of "you owe me" or proportional splitting based on income. If you want to keep finances partly separate while splitting shared costs, YNAB does not help with the splitting part. The price is also the highest on this list.

## 7. Copilot

**Best for:** Couples who want beautiful financial dashboards and AI-powered insights

Copilot is a modern finance tracker with a strong focus on design and AI-driven insights. It connects to bank accounts, categorizes transactions automatically, and provides clean visualizations of your spending patterns.

**Key features:**
- Automatic transaction categorization with AI
- Investment tracking alongside spending
- Subscription detection and management
- Beautiful charts and spending breakdowns
- Recurring transaction identification

**Pricing:** $14.99/month or $119.99/year

**Strengths:** Copilot has the best-looking interface of any finance app. The AI categorization is genuinely good, and the investment tracking is a nice bonus. For couples who want a comprehensive dashboard of their entire financial life, Copilot delivers.

**Limitations:** Like YNAB, Copilot is a personal finance tool, not a couples expense splitting tool. There is no partner sharing, no split calculation, and no settlement tracking. Both partners would need their own subscription and there is no built-in way to see a combined view. It is also iOS-only and the most expensive option here.

## How to Choose the Right App

Your best choice depends on what problem you are actually trying to solve:

- **"We just need to split shared costs fairly."** Melon is purpose-built for this. It handles proportional splitting, running balances, and settlement in a clean, fast interface.
- **"We split costs with friends too, not just each other."** Splitwise handles multi-person group dynamics better than anyone. Use it alongside Melon if you want better couples-specific features.
- **"We want to see all our accounts in one place."** Honeydue or Zeta gives you that aggregated view, though neither handles splitting as well as dedicated tools.
- **"We need to completely overhaul our budgeting."** YNAB is the most transformative option, but you will need a separate tool for expense splitting.
- **"We want a beautiful financial dashboard."** Copilot wins on design and AI features, but it is a personal tool, not a couples tool.

Many couples find that the best solution is combining two apps: one for splitting (like Melon) and one for broader financial planning (like YNAB or Copilot). The key is finding a system you will both actually use consistently.
