Moving in together is one of the most exciting milestones in a relationship. It is also one of the most financially significant. Suddenly you are sharing rent, splitting groceries, deciding who pays the electric bill, and figuring out what happens when one person wants the premium streaming package and the other thinks basic is fine.

Most couples focus on the fun stuff — furniture shopping, decorating, choosing a neighborhood. But the couples who thrive after moving in are the ones who also tackle the financial side head-on, before the first box gets unpacked.

This checklist walks you through everything you need to discuss, decide, and set up. Think of it as your financial moving-in playbook.

## Before You Move: The Money Talk

This is the single most important conversation you will have about cohabitation finances. Ideally, have it at least a month before your move date, when you still have time to plan and are not stressed about logistics.

### Lay Your Financial Cards on the Table

Both partners should share:

- **Your income.** After-tax, take-home pay. If your income is variable, share the range and average.
- **Your debts.** Student loans, credit cards, car payments, personal loans — all of it. No surprises.
- **Your savings.** Emergency fund, retirement accounts, other savings. This is not about judgment — it is about understanding each person's financial starting point.
- **Your credit score.** This matters because landlords check it. If one partner has poor credit, you need to plan for that.
- **Your financial obligations.** Do you send money to family? Have child support? Pay for your own health insurance? These reduce your available income.

This conversation can feel vulnerable, but it sets the foundation for everything that follows. No partnership works well with hidden financial information.

### Discuss Your Money Personalities

You do not need a formal assessment. Just talk honestly about:

- Are you a saver or a spender?
- How do you feel about debt?
- What does financial security mean to you?
- What are your biggest financial goals for the next two years?
- What is a purchase amount that feels significant enough to discuss with your partner before buying?

Understanding each other's money personality helps you build a system that works for both of you, rather than one that constantly creates friction.

### Decide How You Will Handle Shared Finances

There are three main approaches, and none of them is universally right or wrong:

**Fully merged:** All income goes into a joint account. All expenses come out of it. Simple, transparent, but requires deep trust and alignment on spending.

**Fully separate:** Each person maintains their own accounts. You split or take turns paying for shared expenses. Maximum autonomy, but more tracking required.

**Hybrid (most popular):** You each keep individual accounts for personal spending but create a joint system for shared expenses. Both partners contribute an agreed amount — either equal or proportional to income — and shared costs come from the joint pool.

For most couples moving in together for the first time, the hybrid approach offers the best balance of togetherness and independence. You can always adjust later as your relationship and financial situation evolve.

## Your Shared Expenses List

Before you can split expenses, you need to agree on what counts as shared. Here is a comprehensive list to work through together.

### Housing Costs
- Rent or mortgage payment
- Renter's or homeowner's insurance
- Security deposit (who pays, and who gets it back?)
- Parking fees, if applicable

### Utilities and Services
- Electricity
- Gas or heating
- Water and sewer
- Internet
- Trash and recycling (if not included in rent)

### Household Essentials
- Groceries and household supplies (toilet paper, cleaning products, etc.)
- Shared furniture and appliances
- Home maintenance and repairs

### Subscriptions and Entertainment
- Streaming services (decide together which ones you actually need)
- Shared gym membership or fitness classes
- Shared subscriptions (meal kits, news, etc.)

### Transportation (If Shared)
- Car payment on a shared vehicle
- Car insurance
- Gas and maintenance
- Public transit passes

### Costs That Need Discussion
- Pet expenses — whose pet is it, and who pays for food, vet bills, and supplies?
- Guests — if one partner's friends visit frequently, are extra food and utility costs shared?
- Home office expenses — if one person works from home and the other does not, is the extra electricity a shared cost?

Write down every shared expense and the expected monthly amount. Total it up. This is the number you will use to determine each person's contribution.

## Setting Up Your System

You have had the big talk. You know your shared expenses. Now it is time to build the infrastructure.

### Choose Your Splitting Method

The three most common approaches for couples:

- **50/50 split:** Each partner pays exactly half of shared expenses. Simple and straightforward, but only truly fair when incomes are similar.
- **Proportional split:** Each partner pays a percentage based on their share of the combined income. Fairer when there is an income gap. For a detailed walkthrough of this method, read our guide on [splitting expenses fairly when you earn different amounts](/blog/fair-expense-splitting-income-gap).
- **Category ownership:** Each partner "owns" certain expense categories. One pays rent, the other covers utilities and groceries. Less mathematical, but harder to keep balanced.

If your incomes are within 10-15% of each other, 50/50 works fine. If the gap is larger, proportional splitting prevents the lower earner from feeling financially squeezed. Check out our breakdown of the [50/30/20 budget rule for couples](/blog/50-30-20-budget-rule-couples) to see how your shared expenses fit into a broader budget framework.

### Set Up Your Tracking

Pick an expense tracking method and commit to using it from day one. Your options:

- **A couples expense app** like Melon, which is designed specifically for tracking and splitting shared costs between two people. See our [full comparison of apps](/blog/best-expense-splitting-apps-couples) to find the right fit.
- **A shared spreadsheet** if you are both comfortable with Google Sheets or Excel. More manual effort, but fully customizable.
- **A shared note** on your phone for a bare-minimum approach. Better than nothing, but hard to maintain long-term.

Whatever you choose, the key is logging expenses in real time. Trying to reconstruct two weeks of spending from memory is frustrating and inaccurate.

### Open a Joint Account (Optional but Helpful)

If you are going the hybrid route, opening a joint checking account for shared expenses simplifies everything. Each partner sets up automatic transfers into the joint account on payday. All shared bills get paid from it. No chasing reimbursements, no tracking who covered what.

You do not need to put all your money in this account. Just enough to cover shared expenses with a small buffer. Both partners maintain their individual accounts for personal spending, savings, and anything that is not a shared cost.

### Set Up Automatic Payments

For every recurring shared expense that allows it, set up autopay from your joint account (or from whoever is responsible for that bill). This eliminates late fees, reduces the mental load, and means fewer things to track manually.

### Agree on a Settlement Cadence

For expenses that are not on autopay — groceries, dining out, household purchases — agree on how often you will settle up. Weekly tends to work best for couples who are just starting out. It keeps balances small and prevents forgotten expenses. For tips on making settlement comfortable, our guide on [settling up without awkwardness](/blog/settle-up-without-awkwardness) is worth a read.

## First-Month Tips

Your first month living together will be financially unusual. There are one-time costs, unexpected purchases, and a learning curve. Here is how to handle it gracefully.

### Budget for Move-In Costs Separately

Moving expenses (security deposit, first and last month's rent, moving truck, new furniture) are one-time costs. Do not try to fold them into your regular monthly budget. Decide upfront how to split these costs — 50/50 is common even for couples who will use proportional splitting going forward, since these costs benefit both equally.

### Track Everything, Even Small Purchases

In the first month, you will buy things you did not anticipate: a shower curtain, extra hangers, a power strip, kitchen utensils. Log all of these shared purchases, no matter how small. They add up surprisingly fast, and skipping them sets a bad precedent for your tracking habits.

### Do a Two-Week Check-In

After two weeks of living together, sit down and review how the financial system is working. Ask each other:

- Is the expense tracking manageable, or is it already falling behind?
- Do the split percentages feel fair in practice?
- Are there expenses we did not anticipate that should be added to the shared list?
- Is anything about the current system creating stress?

This early check-in catches problems while they are small and easy to fix.

### Give Each Other Grace

One of you will forget to log an expense. One of you will accidentally buy something you said you would not. One of you will spend more than expected on furnishing the living room. This is normal. The first month is about building habits, not achieving perfection.

## Common First-Year Mistakes to Avoid

### Never Talking About Money Again

The move-in conversation is not a one-time event. Your financial situation will change — raises, job changes, new expenses, shifting priorities. Schedule a quarterly money check-in where you review your shared budget, discuss upcoming big expenses, and adjust your system as needed.

### Letting Small Resentments Fester

If something about the financial arrangement bothers you, say it now. "I feel like I have been covering more groceries than we agreed" is a reasonable thing to bring up. It is much easier to address than the same complaint after six months of silence.

### Forgetting About Individual Savings

Shared expenses are important, but do not let them crowd out individual financial goals. Both partners should continue building personal emergency funds, contributing to retirement, and saving for individual goals. A healthy financial partnership supports each person's individual financial health, not just the shared household.

### Ignoring Insurance

Renter's insurance is inexpensive and essential. Make sure you have a policy that covers both of your belongings. Also review health insurance, auto insurance, and life insurance if relevant. Moving in together sometimes opens up new options for coverage.

### Not Discussing the "What If"

Nobody wants to think about breaking up when they are excitedly moving in together. But having a basic agreement about what happens if things do not work out is both mature and protective. Who keeps the apartment? How do you split furniture you bought together? What happens to the security deposit? A brief, calm conversation now can prevent a devastating financial fight later.

## Your Move-In Financial Checklist

Here is everything in one quick-reference list. Check off each item as you go:

- [ ] Have the full financial transparency conversation (income, debt, savings, credit)
- [ ] Discuss money personalities and financial goals
- [ ] Choose a finance model (merged, separate, or hybrid)
- [ ] List all shared expenses and total the monthly cost
- [ ] Decide on a splitting method (50/50, proportional, or category-based)
- [ ] Set up an expense tracking tool
- [ ] Open a joint account (if using the hybrid approach)
- [ ] Set up autopay for recurring shared bills
- [ ] Decide on a settlement schedule for variable expenses
- [ ] Budget for one-time move-in costs separately
- [ ] Plan a two-week financial check-in
- [ ] Get renter's insurance
- [ ] Discuss the "what if we separate" plan
- [ ] Schedule quarterly money check-ins going forward

Moving in with your partner should be joyful, not financially stressful. By taking the time to set up a solid financial foundation now, you free yourselves to focus on what actually matters: building a home together. The money conversations might not be romantic, but they are one of the most caring things you can do for your relationship.
